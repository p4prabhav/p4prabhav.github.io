Critical Reflection

On my shift last week, where I was working as an AIN, I had to sponge a patient who was in palliative care due to a brain tumour. My co-worker had gone to find a lifter, whilst I washed the patient’s face. Her eyes were open and, as I was told that she doesn’t respond much, I assumed that this was normal for her. I closed her eyes to clean them properly, but she just did not open her eyes after that. When I noticed no chest movements and no pulse rate, I immediately told the registered nurse who came and checked the patient’s vital signs. The Registered Nurse then declared that the patient had died. As this was the first time that I encountered death in my life, I felt nervous and scared. I was upset because I felt that the patient died in my care and I could have saved her. However, after speaking with the family I felt better because I could share their grief and console them. AIN is supposed to ensure patient safety and take care of all the needs of the patient. While providing end-of-life care we need to acknowledge the patient’s beliefs and perform ceremonies according to their customs. After experiencing this personally I realised that how important it is to know the person to provide Quality Care. Also, I understood the importance of being prepared for unplanned events like death because it is important to control emotional responses in such situations. If I had read more about the end-of-life care and how to perform different ceremonies according to different religions and ethnicities at the death time I could have handled it more independently and confidently. However, I am sure I will be able to deal with any such experience more appropriately in the future, I will stay calm and emotionally stable. Rather than getting frightened I will immediately inform the team leader and the registered nurse. I will be empathetic towards the family members and give them emotional support. I will respect the rituals that the family will follow and pay my contribution too.
